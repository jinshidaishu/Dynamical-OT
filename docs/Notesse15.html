5.2 Dynamical OT Induces Straight Trajectories

In this section, we discuss in more detail the relations between dynamical OT and static OT.

We first recall the displacement interpolation induced by the static balanced optimal transport problem. Let \(\mu ,\nu \in \mathcal {P}_2(\Omega )\), and consider the quadratic cost \begin {align*} c(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)=\|\mathbf {x}_1-\mathbf {x}_0\|_2^2. \end {align*}

For \(t\in [0,1]\), define the interpolation map on pairs of points by \begin {align} \label {eqn:linear_interpolation_map} \mathbf {e}_t(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)=(1-t)\mathbf {x}_0+t\mathbf {x}_1. \end {align}

This map moves a point \(\mathbf {x}_0\) to a point \(\mathbf {x}_1\) along the constant-speed straight line segment joining them.

Proposition 5.2.1 (Displacement interpolation: Kantorovich form). Let \(\pi ^*\in \Pi (\mu ,\nu )\) be an optimal coupling for the quadratic cost. Then the curve \begin {align} \label {eqn:displacement_interpolation_kantorovich} \rho _t=(\mathbf {e}_t)_\sharp \pi ^*, \qquad 0\le t\le 1, \end {align}

is a constant-speed \(W_2\)-geodesic connecting \(\mu \) and \(\nu \). Equivalently, for any test function \(\psi \), \begin {align} \label {eqn:displacement_interpolation_weak} \int _{\Omega }\psi (\mathbf {x})\,\mathrm {d}\rho _t(\mathbf {x}) =\int _{\Omega \times \Omega } \psi \big ((1-t)\mathbf {x}_0+t\mathbf {x}_1\big )\,\mathrm {d}\pi ^*(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1). \end {align}

Proof. The formula is simply the push-forward definition of \((\mathbf {e}_t)_\sharp \pi ^*\). The endpoint conditions follow from the two marginals of \(\pi ^*\): since \(\mathbf {e}_0(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)=\mathbf {x}_0\) and \(\mathbf {e}_1(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)=\mathbf {x}_1\), we have \(\rho _0=\mu \) and \(\rho _1=\nu \). The optimality of \(\pi ^*\) gives \begin {align*} W_2^2(\mu ,\nu )=\int _{\Omega \times \Omega }\|\mathbf {x}_1-\mathbf {x}_0\|_2^2\,\mathrm {d}\pi ^*(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1), \end {align*}

and the path \(t\mapsto \mathbf {e}_t(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)\) has constant speed \(\|\mathbf {x}_1-\mathbf {x}_0\|_2\). Hence the induced curve has length equal to \(W_2(\mu ,\nu )\), and therefore it is a constant-speed Wasserstein geodesic. □

Another way to understand this result is the following. Write \(\rho _0=\mu \) and \(\rho _1=\nu \), and let \(\pi ^*\) be an optimal coupling between \(\rho _0\) and \(\rho _1\). Each infinitesimal mass element \(\mathrm {d}\pi ^*(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)\) is transported from \(\mathbf {x}_0\) to \(\mathbf {x}_1\). For the quadratic transport cost, the least-energy path between these two points is the constant-speed straight line \begin {align*} \mathbf {X}_t(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)=(1-t)\mathbf {x}_0+t\mathbf {x}_1. \end {align*}

Therefore, at time \(t\), the mass \(\mathrm {d}\pi ^*(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)\) is located at \(\mathbf {X}_t(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1)\). Summing over all pairs gives \begin {align*} \rho _t=\int _{\Omega \times \Omega } \delta _{(1-t)\mathbf {x}_0+t\mathbf {x}_1}\, \mathrm {d}\pi ^*(\mathbf {x}_0,\mathbf {x}_1). \end {align*}

In the discrete case, if \begin {align*} \pi ^*=\sum _{i,j}\pi _{ij}\delta _{(\mathbf {x}_i,\mathbf {y}_j)}, \end {align*}

then \begin {align*} \rho _t=\sum _{i,j}\pi _{ij}\delta _{(1-t)\mathbf {x}_i+t\mathbf {y}_j}. \end {align*}

Thus each mass packet \(\pi _{ij}\) moves from \(\mathbf {x}_i\) to \(\mathbf {y}_j\) along a straight line.

Corollary 5.2.2 (Displacement interpolation: Monge form). Assume that an optimal transport map \(\mathbf {T}^*: \Omega \rightarrow \Omega \) exists, namely \(\mathbf {T}^*_\sharp \mu =\nu \), and that the optimal coupling is concentrated on its graph: \begin {align*} \pi ^*=(\id ,\mathbf {T}^*)_\sharp \mu . \end {align*}

Then the displacement interpolation in Proposition 5.2.1 becomes \begin {align} \label {eqn:displacement_interpolation_monge} \rho _t=\big ((1-t)\id +t\mathbf {T}^*\big )_\sharp \mu . \end {align}

Equivalently, each particle initially located at \(\mathbf {x}\) follows \begin {align} \label {eqn:displacement_interpolation_particle} \mathbf {X}_t(\mathbf {x})=(1-t)\mathbf {x}+t\mathbf {T}^*(\mathbf {x}), \qquad \dot {\mathbf {X}}_t(\mathbf {x})=\mathbf {T}^*(\mathbf {x})-\mathbf {x}. \end {align}

Proof. Substitute \(\pi ^*=(\id ,\mathbf {T}^*)_\sharp \mu \) into the Kantorovich displacement interpolation formula. Then \begin {align*} (\mathbf {e}_t)_\sharp \pi ^* =\big (\mathbf {e}_t\circ (\id ,\mathbf {T}^*)\big )_\sharp \mu =\big ((1-t)\id +t\mathbf {T}^*\big )_\sharp \mu , \end {align*}

which proves the Monge displacement interpolation formula. The particle formula follows directly from the definition of the map \((1-t)\id +t\mathbf {T}^*\). □

Remark 5.2.3. The Kantorovich formula is the general one: it allows the optimal plan to split mass. The Monge formula is the special case in which the optimal plan is induced by a map. In both cases the total mass is conserved and the interpolation describes the balanced \(W_2\)-geodesic. The Benamou–Brenier formulation gives the Eulerian description of the same curve through the continuity equation.

We next show that the Benamou-Brenier OT formulation (5.3) can be reformulated as a convex problem with linear constraint as follows \begin {align} \label {eqn:dynamicalOT03} C_{\mathrm {BB}}(\mu ,\nu ) = &\min _{(\rho _t,\mathbf {m}_t)\in \mathcal {C}_{\mathrm {BB}}}\ A[\rho _t, \mathbf {m}_t] = \int _0^1\int _{\Omega } J(\rho _t, \mathbf {m}_t) \ \dx \dt , \end {align}

with linearly constrained feasible set \begin {align} \mathcal {C}_{\mathrm {BB}}(\mu ,\nu ) : = \left \{ (\rho _t,\mathbf {m}_t) |\ \partial _t\rho _t + \nabla \cdot \mathbf {m}_t = 0,\ \rho (0,\mathbf {x})=\mu ,\ \rho (1,\mathbf {x})=\nu ,\ \mathbf {m}\cdot \mathbf {n}|_{\partial \Omega } = 0 \right \}. \end {align}

Here \(J\) is the lower semi-continuous convex function \begin {align} \label {eqn:J_definition02} J(\rho _t,\mathbf {m}_t) = \begin {cases} \rho _t^{-1}\|\mathbf {m}_t\|_2^2 \quad &\text {if\ } \rho >0, \\ 0 \quad &\text {if\ } (\rho _t, \mathbf {m}_t) = (0,0), \\ +\infty , \quad & \text {otherwise}. \end {cases} \end {align}

Here we employ a slight abuse of notation with \(A[\rho _t,\mathbf {m}_t]\) and \(\mathcal {C}(\mu ,\nu )\). This rewrite is crucial for the numerical treatment for the Benamou-Brenier OT (5.3). Besides, it is also the key for proving the existence of the minimizers for Benamou-Brenier.

Now we can present the dual formulation for the Benamou-Brenier problem.

Proposition 5.2.4 (Dual form of Benamou-Brenier). The minimizer \((\rho ,\mathbf {v})\) of the Benamou-Brenier dynamical OT (5.3) satisfies \begin {align*} \mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {x}) = \nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {x}) \end {align*}

in which the dual variable \(\Phi (t,\mathbf {x})\) is determined by \begin {align} \label {eqn:BB_dualsystem} \begin {cases} \partial _t\rho (t,\mathbf {x}) + \nabla \cdot (\rho (t,\mathbf {x})\nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {x})) = 0, \\ \partial _t\Phi (t,\mathbf {x}) + \dfrac {1}{2}\|\nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {x})\|_2^2 \le 0, \\ \rho (0,\mathbf {x}) = \mu , \quad \rho (1,\mathbf {x}) = \nu . \end {cases} \end {align}

If \(\rho (t,\mathbf {x})>0\), then \begin {align} \label {eqn:HJ02} \partial _t\Phi (t,\mathbf {x}) + \frac {1}{2}\|\nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {x})\|_2^2 = 0. \end {align}

Proof. The proof is straightforward. Taking \(2\Phi _t(\mathbf {x}) = 2\Phi (t,\mathbf {x})\) as the dual variable and considering the Lagrangian \begin {align*} \mathcal {L}(\rho ,\mathbf {m};\Phi ) = \int _0^1\int _{\Omega } \rho _t^{-1}\|\mathbf {m}\|_2^2 + 2\Phi _t\Big (\partial _t\rho (t,\mathbf {x})+\nabla \cdot \mathbf {m}(t,\mathbf {x})\Big )\ \dx \dt . \end {align*}

By the conditions \begin {align*} &\frac {\delta \mathcal {L}}{\delta \rho }\ge 0 \Longrightarrow -\rho _t^{-2}\|\mathbf {m}\|_2^2 - 2\partial _t\Phi _t \ge 0 ; \\ &\frac {\delta \mathcal {L}}{\delta \mathbf {m}}=0 \Longrightarrow -2\rho _t^{-1}\mathbf {m} -2 \nabla \Phi _t = 0; \\ &\frac {\delta \mathcal {L}}{\delta \Phi }=0 \Longrightarrow \partial _t\rho (t,\mathbf {x})+\nabla \cdot \mathbf {m}(t,\mathbf {x}), \end {align*}

which recover the required system by using \(\nabla \Phi _t = \rho ^{-1}\mathbf {m} = \mathbf {v}\). If \(\rho _t >0\) strictly, the Hamilton-Jacobi equation has equal sign. □

Now we can provide an alternative derivation of the static Monge formulation from the Benamou-Brenier (5.3).

Proposition 5.2.5. Given the following static Monge OT \begin {align} C_{\mathrm {M}}(\mu ,\nu ) : = \inf \left \{\int _{\Omega } \mu (\mathbf {x}) \|\mathbf {T}(\mathbf {x}) - \mathbf {x}\|_2^2 \ \dx \mid \mathbf {T}_\sharp \mu = \nu \right \}, \end {align}

we have that \begin {align*} C_{\mathrm {M}}(\mu ,\nu ) = C_{\mathrm {BB}}(\mu ,\nu ). \end {align*}

Proof. Consider any (smooth) velocity field and its flow \((\mathbf {v}, \mathbf {X})\) satisfying \begin {align} \label {eqn:dynamicalOT_velocity_flow02} \begin {cases} \dot {\mathbf {X}}(t, \mathbf {x}) = \mathbf {v}(t, \mathbf {X}(t, \mathbf {x})), \\ \mathbf {X}(0, \mathbf {x}) = \mathbf {x}, \end {cases} \end {align}

and define \(\rho (t,\mathbf {x}) := (\mathbf {X}_t)_{\sharp }\mu (\mathbf {x})\). By Lemma 5.1.1, \((\rho ,\mathbf {v})\) satisfies the continuity equation (5.2). Then we have \begin {align} \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \rho (t,\mathbf {x}) \|\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {x})\|_2^2 \ \dx \dt &= \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \rho (t,\mathbf {X}) \|\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X})\|_2^2 \ \mathrm {d}\mathbf {X}\dt \nonumber \\ &= \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \|\dot {\mathbf {X}}(t,\mathbf {x})\|_2^2 \ \rho (t,\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {d}\mathbf {x}\dt . \label {eqn:CM_CBB_temp} \end {align}

We next derive a differential equation for the quantity \(J(t,\mathbf {x}) = \rho (t,\mathbf {X}_t(\mathbf {x})) \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}_t(\mathbf {x}))\). Note that \begin {align*} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d}t}J(t,\mathbf {x}) & = \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d}t} \Big [ \rho (t,\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \Big ] \\ & = \partial _t\rho (t,\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) + \nabla _{\mathbf {X}} \rho (t,\mathbf {X}) \frac {\mathrm {d}\mathbf {X}}{\mathrm {d}t} \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) + \rho (t,\mathbf {X}) \partial _t\Big [ \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \Big ] \\ & = \Big ( \partial _t\rho + \nabla \rho \cdot \mathbf {v} + \rho \nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} \Big )(t,\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \\ & = \Big ( \partial _t\rho + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf {v}) \Big )(t,\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) = 0, \end {align*}

where the third equality is due to the Jacobi’s formula: \begin {align*} \partial _t \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) & = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {tr}\left ( (\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X})^{-1} \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d}t}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \right ) = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {tr}\left ( (\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X})^{-1} \nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\frac {\mathrm {d}\mathbf {X}}{\mathrm {d}t} \right ) \\ & = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {tr}\left ( (\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X})^{-1} \nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X}) \right ) = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {tr}\left ( (\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X})^{-1} \nabla _{\mathbf {X}}\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X}) \nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X} \right ) \\ & = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {tr}\left ( \nabla _{\mathbf {X}}\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X}) \nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X} (\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X})^{-1} \right ) = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \mathrm {tr}\left ( \nabla _{\mathbf {X}}\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X}) \right ) \\ & = \mathrm {det}(\nabla _{\mathbf {x}}\mathbf {X}) \nabla _{\mathbf {X}}\cdot \mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X}) \end {align*}

Therefore we have \( J(t,\mathbf {x}) = J(0,\mathbf {x}) = \rho (0,\mathbf {x}) = \mu (\mathbf {x}). \) When \(\mathbf {X}\) is the Lagrangian minimizer, the minimizer in Eulerian coordinates satisfies the Hamilton-Jacobi equation (5.11). This combined with \(\dot {\mathbf {X}}=\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X})=\nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {X})\), we have that \begin {align*} \ddot {\mathbf {X}}(t,\mathbf {x}) = \frac {\mathrm {d}}{\mathrm {d}t}\nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {X}) = \partial _t \nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {X}) + \nabla ^2\Phi (t,\mathbf {X}) \frac {\mathrm {d}\mathbf {X}}{\mathrm {d}t} = \nabla _{\mathbf {X}} \partial _t\Phi (t,\mathbf {X}) + \nabla _{\mathbf {X}}\Big ( \frac {1}{2}\| \nabla \Phi (t,\mathbf {X}) \|_2^2 \Big ) = 0. \end {align*}

This implies that \(\dot {\mathbf {X}}\) is a constant with respect to \(t\) and \begin {align*} \dot {\mathbf {X}}=\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {X}) = \int _0^1 \dot {\mathbf {X}}\ \dt = \mathbf {X}(1,\mathbf {x}) - \mathbf {X}(0,\mathbf {x}) := \mathbf {T}(\mathbf {x}) - \mathbf {x}. \end {align*}

It follows that \begin {align*} \mathbf {X}(t,\mathbf {x}) = t\mathbf {T}(\mathbf {x}) + (1-t)\mathbf {x}. \end {align*}

Substituting the above equations into (5.14), we have \begin {align*} \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \rho (t,\mathbf {x}) \|\mathbf {v}(t,\mathbf {x})\|_2^2 \ \dx \dt & = \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \|\dot {\mathbf {X}}(t,\mathbf {x})\|_2^2 \ J(t,\mathbf {x})\mathrm {d}\mathbf {x}\dt \\ & = \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \|\mathbf {T}(\mathbf {x})-\mathbf {x}\|_2^2 \ J(0,\mathbf {x})\mathrm {d}\mathbf {x}\dt \\ & = \int _0^1 \int _{\Omega } \|\mathbf {T}(\mathbf {x})-\mathbf {x}\|_2^2 \ \mu (\mathbf {x})\mathrm {d}\mathbf {x}\dt . \end {align*}

Proof is completed. □
